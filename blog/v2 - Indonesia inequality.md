Lately, we can hear a lot of complaints from Indonesian netizens about how increasingly hard it is, economically, to survive everyday life. While the middle class are getting angry about rising prices, a report from Celios came and tell us that 50 richest people in Indonesia own an equal amount of wealth as 55 million Indonesian.

We certainly *feel* the economic struggles and accelerating inequality happening around us but unfortunately this is not something easy to know for sure. I personally believe that human’s feeling is actually a very strong radar to sense reality. So being curious, I dig into the data. 

Before going into my personal observations, I would like to tell you my approach. Even though the increasing concern about rising prices and inequality is relatively recent (supported by the recent Indonesian political and economic situation), I tried to look at this on a longer time horizon to see whether this is a newer concern or we were just not aware of it earlier. In order to come up with the conclusion, I focus my analysis on these main questions:
1. Is Indonesia getting richer over time?
2. Are Indonesians people’s lives getting better over time?
3. Is the advancement in welfare felt equally by the poor and the rich?

**Indonesia, the country, is getting richer over time.** Our real GDP per capita, which means GDP per capita after adjusting for inflation, consistently grow throughout the year with 2020 as an understandable exception (I kinda miss the unlimited work from home quota). So, our country is getting richer (good news) but how does this translate to the welfare of the people? 

![[Pasted image 20260516215600.png]]
<p align="center">Figure 1. Indonesia real GDP per capita adjusted with inflation</p>
**Indonesians, the people, are getting richer over time.** Our wages growth from the year 2000 to 2024 beat the price increase by considerable amount. This means Indonesian real wage is now at a better position compared to 26 years ago. Comparing real wage in this 24 year long horizon is not so easy because current 35 year old adults were just little kids with totally different financial point of view. However, I find an arbitrary indicator to sense check this data. Now, in 2026, more people you know have travelled abroad compared to 24 years ago. 
![[Pasted image 20260516224304.png]]
<p align="center">Figure 2. Indonesia nominal wages, prices, and purchasing power</p>
**The richer you are, the better your life improvement was.** Good news for the rich. We can see from Figure 3 that all segments of people, from the very rich to the very poor, has increased their average consumption (proxy for welfare) significantly throughout the year. However, the interesting thing is the richer you are, the faster your "welfare" grow. Does this mean inequality is getting higher?
![[Pasted image 20260516225135.png]]
<p align="center">Figure 3. Nominal average consumption by decile indexed to 2000</p>
**Yes, Indonesia is more unequal today compared to 26 years ago.** We can see from Figure 4 that Indonesia's [gini index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gini_coefficient) has increased from 30 to 35 in the span of 34 years. Gini index is used to measure wealth inequality in a particular area of interest. 0 means everyone has the same income/wealth/consumptions while 100 means all income/wealth/consumptions are concentrated only in 1 person out of the population. We can see that Indonesia's gini index decreased from the peak year of 2013 until 2024 but this will be out of this blog scope.
![[Pasted image 20260516225946.png]]
<p align=center>Figure 4. Indonesia gini index</p>
**Indonesia is not the most unequal country in the world.** If we only hear about Indonesia inequality from domestic news it can feel like we experience the worst kind of inequality. However, data (Figure 5) says that we are certainly not the most unequal country in the world, but we are also not the most equal. Regarding this, I don't say that we shouldn't aim for more equality. I certainly agree that we should further reduce our gini to improve welfare of the masses.

![[Pasted image 20260516231001.png]]
<p align=center>Figure 5. Gini index comparison among countries (latest available year)</p>
Seeing the data in the past 24 years seems pretty positive except for the inequality increase. But how is the situation in the more recent years?

**Wage cannot keep up with price increase in the year 2020-2023.** Figure 6 shows that while average wage increased by 8.4%, total CPI (Consumer Price Index: indicator of price paid by consumer) increased by 9.7%. This means real wage actually decrease and cannot keep up with price. Price increase was more apparent in transport, food and beverage, and restaurants which are near and dear to us.
![[Pasted image 20260516234711.png]]
<p align=center>Figure 6. National average wage vs category CPI increase</p>
**However, one good news is the increase of wage is concentrated on the bottom decile.** This means the welfare of the very poor is still increasing despite average contractions in terms of real wage.
![[Pasted image 20260517000009.png]]
<p align=center>Figure 7. Average wage growth by decile 2020-2023</p>
**This drop in real wage forced middle class to be careful with their spending/consumptions.** Figure 8 shows that decile 50% until 90% consumptions growth are way below national average. Anomaly is found on the top 10% where their average consumption increase significantly despite drop in real wage. This will be an interesting study to conduct in the future.
