# Critique and Improvement Notes for `v2 - Indonesia inequality.md`

## Core Assessment

The argument is directionally interesting, but currently too easy to attack because it mixes **wealth, income, wages, consumption, welfare, and inequality** as if they are interchangeable.

The strongest version of the article should say:

> Indonesia became richer over the long run, and average real wages/consumption improved. But the gains were uneven, inequality rose compared with 2000, and the recent 2020-2023 period helps explain why many people still feel squeezed despite long-run improvement.

That is defensible. Some current claims are not.

## Biggest Vulnerabilities

### 1. Opening Wealth Claim Is Not Directly Tested

Current issue:

The blog opens with the Celios claim that the 50 richest Indonesians own as much wealth as 55 million Indonesians. But most charts use **GDP, employee wages, CPI, income/consumption Gini, and consumption**.

Why this is vulnerable:

Someone can say: "Your evidence does not test your opening claim."

Suggested fix:

Frame Celios as context only, then say clearly that the article focuses on **income/consumption inequality**, not wealth inequality.

Possible wording:

> The Celios figure is about wealth concentration. This post does not directly test wealth inequality. Instead, I look at GDP, wages, prices, consumption, and income/consumption inequality to understand whether ordinary economic welfare has improved and whether the gains were shared evenly.

### 2. "Feelings Are Radar" Is Easy to Attack

Current issue:

The draft says human feeling is a strong radar to sense reality.

Why this is vulnerable:

It sounds anecdotal and can make the piece feel less evidence-based before the analysis starts.

Suggested fix:

Soften it.

Possible wording:

> Feelings are not proof, but they are a useful starting point for asking what the data says.

### 3. "Indonesians Are Getting Richer" Is Too Broad

Current issue:

The section says Indonesians are getting richer over time based on average wage growth.

Why this is vulnerable:

Figure 2 measures **average employee wages**, not all Indonesians. It may exclude non-workers, unemployed people, some informal workers depending on source coverage, household-size effects, and asset income.

Suggested fix:

Make the claim narrower.

Possible wording:

> Average employee wages have risen faster than CPI since 2000.

or:

> For employees captured in the wage data, purchasing power is higher than it was in 2000.

### 4. Wage Period Should Be 2000-2023, Not 2000-2024

Current issue:

The text says wage growth from 2000 to 2024.

Why this is vulnerable:

The processed wage data has no 2024 wage observation. It ends at 2023.

Suggested fix:

Use **2000-2023** for wage analysis.

Possible wording:

> From 2000 to 2023, average employee wages rose faster than consumer prices.

### 5. Abroad Travel Anecdote Weakens the Evidence

Current issue:

The draft uses "more people you know have travelled abroad" as a sense check.

Why this is vulnerable:

This is anecdotal and class-biased. It may reflect the author's social circle more than national living standards.

Suggested fix:

Remove it, or clearly label it as personal intuition rather than evidence.

Better option:

Delete the sentence entirely and let Figure 2 carry the point.

### 6. Consumption, Welfare, and Nominal Indexes Need Cleaner Wording

Current issue:

Figure 3 is titled "Nominal Average Consumption by Decile Indexed to 2000," but the text says "life improvement" and "welfare."

Why this is vulnerable:

The notebook says PIP real consumption welfare was converted into implied nominal indexes using CPI. That is not the same as directly observing total welfare.

Suggested fix:

Use "measured consumption" or "consumption-based welfare," and explain the limitation.

Possible wording:

> Figure 3 uses consumption-based welfare data by decile. It suggests that consumption improved across the distribution, but higher deciles saw faster gains.

Avoid:

> The richer you are, the better your life improvement was.

Stronger version:

> Consumption gains were larger for richer groups.

### 7. Gini Is Not Wealth Inequality Here

Current issue:

The draft says Gini measures wealth inequality.

Why this is vulnerable:

The World Bank Gini series used here measures inequality in **income or consumption**, depending on the survey metadata. It is not a direct wealth inequality measure.

Suggested fix:

Possible wording:

> The Gini index here measures inequality in income or consumption, not wealth.

Also adjust the explanation:

> A value of 0 means everyone has the same measured income or consumption; a value of 100 means all measured income or consumption is concentrated in one person.

### 8. The Time Span Is Wrong: 24 Years, Not 34 Years

Current issue:

The draft says Gini increased from 30 to 35 in the span of 34 years.

Why this is vulnerable:

The chart starts in 2000 and ends in 2024. That is 24 years.

Suggested fix:

Possible wording:

> Indonesia's Gini rose from 30.3 in 2000 to 34.9 in 2024.

### 9. "Not the Most Unequal Country" Can Sound Like Minimizing

Current issue:

The text says Indonesia is not the most unequal country in the world.

Why this is vulnerable:

It is true, but the framing can sound like the problem is being dismissed.

Suggested fix:

Reframe the comparative point.

Possible wording:

> Indonesia is not an extreme global outlier, but it is more unequal than several Asian and advanced-country benchmarks.

This is more precise and harder to attack.

### 10. Bottom-Decile Wage Growth Does Not Prove Poor Welfare Improved

Current issue:

The draft says bottom-decile wage growth means welfare of the very poor is still increasing.

Why this is vulnerable:

Figure 7 shows estimated labour income growth by decile. It does not prove total household welfare improved. Welfare also depends on employment status, hours worked, household size, transfers, debt, food inflation, health costs, rent, and non-wage income.

Suggested fix:

Possible wording:

> Estimated labour income rose fastest in the bottom wage deciles, but this does not fully prove total household welfare improved.

or:

> This suggests some wage gains at the bottom, although it should not be read as a complete welfare measure.

### 11. Middle-Class Consumption Pressure Is Not Causally Proven

Current issue:

The draft says the drop in real wage "forced" the middle class to be careful with spending.

Why this is vulnerable:

Figure 8 shows consumption growth by decile. It does not prove why consumption slowed, or that wage pressure caused it.

Suggested fix:

Possible wording:

> This is consistent with middle deciles facing pressure, but the chart alone does not prove causality.

or:

> Middle deciles show weaker consumption growth, which fits the broader story of pressure from prices and wages, though more evidence is needed to prove the mechanism.

## Recommended Revised Thesis

Use this as the backbone:

> The long-run story is not that Indonesia is simply getting poorer. Real GDP per capita rose, average employee wages rose faster than prices, and consumption improved across the distribution. But the gains were not equal. Inequality is higher than in 2000, the top groups captured faster consumption growth, and since 2020 average wages have failed to keep up with CPI. This combination explains why people can feel squeezed even in a country that is richer than before.

## Most Important Addition

Add a short methodology/caveat paragraph before the first chart:

> This post does not measure wealth inequality directly. I use GDP per capita, average wages, CPI, World Bank Gini, and consumption distribution data as proxies for different parts of economic welfare. These indicators do not describe the same thing, so I treat them separately: GDP for national output, wages for employee purchasing power, consumption for living standards, and Gini for income/consumption inequality.

This one paragraph would remove most of the logical vulnerability.

## Suggested Structural Rewrite

The blog will be stronger if the structure becomes:

1. Start with public concern and the Celios wealth claim as context.
2. Clarify that the post analyzes income, wage, price, and consumption data, not wealth directly.
3. Show long-run national growth: real GDP per capita.
4. Show long-run employee purchasing power: real wages.
5. Show long-run distribution: consumption gains by decile and Gini.
6. Then pivot to recent pressure: 2020-2023 wages versus CPI.
7. End with the nuance: Indonesia is richer than before, but gains were uneven and recent price pressure explains why people may still feel worse off.

## Language to Avoid

Avoid these because they overclaim:

- "Indonesians are getting richer."
- "The richer you are, the better your life improvement was."
- "Gini measures wealth inequality."
- "This proves the welfare of the poor improved."
- "This drop in real wage forced the middle class..."

Use these instead:

- "Average employee wages increased faster than CPI."
- "Consumption gains were larger for richer groups."
- "The Gini series here measures income or consumption inequality."
- "This suggests, but does not fully prove..."
- "This is consistent with..."

## Bottom Line

The safest argument is not "Indonesia is getting poorer" or "Indonesia is fine." The strongest argument is:

> Indonesia is materially richer than it was in 2000, but the gains have been uneven, and recent wage-price pressure helps explain why many people feel squeezed despite long-run progress.
